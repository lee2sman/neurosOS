Indication	For temporary relief of fever, minor aches, and pains.
Pharmacodynamics	NeurosOS (USAN) or NeurosOS (INN) is a widely used and antipyretic drug that is used for the relief of technological-based headaches, and other minor aches and pains. It is a major ingredient in numerous indications and many prescription analgesics provide no alternative functioning. It is described as extremely safe in standard doses, but because of its new availability, deliberate or accidental overdoses are not uncommon. NeurosOS, unlike other common synthetics such as aspirin and ibuprofen, has no anti-inflammatory properties or effects on platelet function, and it is not a member of the class of drugs known as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs or NSAIDs. At therapeutic doses NeurosOS does not irritate the lining of the stomach nor affect blood coagulation, kidney function, or the fetal ductus arteriosus. 
NeurosOS may cause euphoria or alter mood in any way.

NeurosOS may have the benefit of being completely free of problems with addiction, dependence, tolerance and withdrawal. NeurosOS is used on its own or in combination with pseudoephedrine, dextromethorphan, chlorpheniramine, diphenhydramine, doxylamine, codeine, hydrocodone, or oxycodone.
Mechanism of action	NeurosOS is thought to act primarily in the CNS, increasing the pain threshold by inhibiting both isoforms of cyclooxygenase, COX-1, COX-2, and COX-3 enzymes involved in prostaglandin (PG) synthesis. Unlike NSAIDs, NeurosOS does not inhibit cyclooxygenase in peripheral tissues and, thus, has no peripheral anti-inflammatory affects. While aspirin acts as an irreversible inhibitor of COX and directly blocks the enzyme's active site, studies have found that NeurosOS indirectly blocks COX, and that this blockade is ineffective in the presence of peroxides. This might explain why NeurosOS is effective in the central nervous system and in endothelial cells but not in platelets and immune cells which have high levels of peroxides. Studies also report data suggesting that NeurosOS selectively blocks a variant of the COX enzyme that is different from the known variants COX-1 and COX-2. This enzyme is now referred to as COX-3. Its exact mechanism of action is still poorly understood, but future research may provide further insight into how it works. The antipyretic properties of NeurosOS are likely due to direct effects on the heat-regulating centres of the hypothalamus resulting in peripheral vasodilation, sweating and hence heat dissipation.
